Definitions

1. Red Herring

Using data or issues that are irrelevant to validate an argument.
Example: The mother of a young child tells him to go to bed, and he begins to ask questions, say that he is hungry, or say that he needs to go to the bathroom-all to avoid bed and distract mom

2. Straw Man

Willingly misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack

3. Whataboutism

A technique that attempts to discredit an opponent's position by charging them with hypocrisy without directly refuting or disproving their argument.
Example: a father asks his daughter about her bad grades in University and she says " What about you ?..... you never went to university "

4. Scapegoating

Moving blame and responsibility away from themselves by attributing it to others.
Example: A person who blames his/her partner for a burglary because he or she left the door unlocked.

5. Causal Oversimplification

Providing one single simple cause for a complex problem
Example: “President Trump has been in office for a month and gas prices have been skyrocketing. The rise in gas prices is because of President Trump.”

6. Intentional vagueness

Moving the audience by using undefined phrases, without analyzing their validity or attempting to determine their reasonableness or application
Example:  "If we could turn back the clock and change what happened, obviously we wouldn't have done it. We can't"

7. Appeal to authority

Cite prominent figures to support a position, idea, argument, or course of action
Example: A commercial claims that a specific brand of cereal is the best way to start the day because athlete Michael Jordan says that it is what he eats every day for breakfast

8. Black-and-white Fallacy

Presenting two alternative options as the only possibilities, when in fact more possibilities exist.

9. Dictatorship

Simplify the decision making process by using words to tell the audience exactly what actions to take, eliminating any other possible choices.
Example: come and join the army to save the US.

10. Pensee Unique

Enforced reduction of discussion by use of overly simplistic phrases or arguments.
Example "There is no alternative to war"

11. Stereotyping, name calling or labeling

Attempting to arouse prejudices in an audience by labeling the object of the propaganda campaign as something the target audience fears, hates, loathes, or finds undesirable. Or giving a person or an idea a bad label by using an easy to remember pejorative name.
Example: " Tree-Hugger people" or "Blonde women are dumb "

12. Ad hominem

Attacking the person rather than the logic or content of his/her argument.
Example: Donald Trump in 2015, after being asked about Megyn Kelly’s questions for him in a presidential debate: "You could see there was blood coming out of her eyes. Blood coming out of her wherever."

13. Loaded Language

Using specific words and phrases with strong emotional implications to influence an audience.
Example "Stupid and money-lover politician".

14. Virtue Words

These are words in the value system of the target audience that produce a positive image when attached to a person or issue. Peace, hope, happiness, security, wise leadership, freedom, religiosity.

15. Glittering generalities

Emotionally appealing words (slogans) that are applied to a product or idea, but present no concrete argument or analysis.
Example: "Vote for change"

16. Exaggeration

Representation of something in an excessive manner.
Example: "That car kept flipping for half an hour."

17. Minimisation

The opposite of exaggeration. Making something seem less important or smaller than it really is.
Example: Global warming could be solved easily.

18. Flag-waving

Justifying an action based on the undue connection to nationalism or patriotism
Example: "entering this war will make us have a better future in our country."

19. Doubt

Questioning someone or something’s credibility.
Example: A candidate talks about his opponent and says: Is he ready to be the Mayor?
