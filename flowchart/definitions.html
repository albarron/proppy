Definitions

1. Presenting Irrelevant Data (Red Herring)

Using data or issues that are irrelevant to validate an argument.
Example: The mother of a young child tells him to go to bed, and he begins to ask questions, say that he is hungry, or say that he needs to go to the bathroom-all to avoid bed and distract mom

2. Misrepresentation of Somenone's Position

Example: Student tells his professor that he thinks some of Donald Trump's positions have merit. Professor says he can't believe that the student believes in support racism

3. Whataboutism

A technique that attempts to discredit an opponent's position by charging them with hypocrisy without directly refuting or disproving their argument.
Example: a father asks his daughter about her bad grades in University and she says " What about you ?..... you never went to university "

4. Associating Guilt to Someone/Something

Moving blame and responsibility away from themselves by attributing it to others.
Example: A person who blames his/her partner for a burglary because he or she left the door unlocked.

5. Causal Oversimplification

Providing one single simple cause for a complex problem
Example: “President Trump has been in office for a month and gas prices have been skyrocketing. The rise in gas prices is because of President Trump.”

6. Obfuscation, Intentional vagueness, Confusion

Moving the audience by using undefined phrases, without analyzing their validity or attempting to determine their reasonableness or application
Example: "If we could turn back the clock and change what happened, obviously we wouldn't have done it. We can't"

7. Appeal to authority

Cite prominent figures to support a position, idea, argument, or course of action
Example: A commercial claims that a specific brand of cereal is the best way to start the day because athlete Michael Jordan says that it is what he eats every day for breakfast

8. Black-and-white Fallacy

Presenting two alternative options as the only possibilities, when in fact more possibilities exist.

9. Dictatorship

Simplify the decision making process by using words to tell the audience exactly what actions to take, eliminating any other possible choices.
Example: come and join the army to save the US.

10. Pensée Unique

Enforced reduction of discussion by use of overly simplistic phrases or arguments.
Example "There is no alternative to war"

11. Stereotyping, name calling or labeling

Attempting to arouse prejudices in an audience by labeling the object of the propaganda campaign as something the target audience fears, hates, loathes, or finds undesirable. Or giving a person or an idea a bad label by using an easy to remember pejorative name.
Example: " Tree-Hugger people" or "Blonde women are dumb "

12. Ad hominem

Attacking the person rather than the logic or content of his/her argument.
Example: Donald Trump in 2015, after being asked about Megyn Kelly’s questions for him in a presidential debate: "You could see there was blood coming out of her eyes. Blood coming out of her wherever."

13. Loaded Language

Using specific words and phrases with strong emotional implications to influence an audience.
Example "Stupid and money-lover politician".

14. Emotional Words

Using words in the value system of the target audience that produce a positive image when attached to a person or issue (peace, hope, happiness, security, wise leadership, freedom, religiosity), but present no concrete argument or analysis.
Example: "Vote for change"

15. Exaggeration

Representation of something in an excessive manner.
Example: "That car kept flipping for half an hour."

16. Minimisation

The opposite of exaggeration. Making something seem less important or smaller than it really is.
Example: Global warming could be solved easily.

17. Flag-waving

Justifying an action based on the undue connection to nationalism or patriotism
Example: "entering this war will make us have a better future in our country."

18. Doubt

Questioning someone or something’s credibility.
Example: A candidate talks about his opponent and says: Is he ready to be the Mayor?

19. Appeal to fear

A technique that seeks to build support by instilling anxieties and panic in the general population
Example: Joseph Goebbels exploited "Theodore Kaufman's Germany Must Perish!"

20. Appeal to prejudice

Using loaded or emotive terms to attach value or moral goodness to believing the proposition.
Example: Right thinking americans will agree with me that we should have another free vote on capital punishment.

21. Slogans

A slogan is a brief, striking phrase that may include labeling and stereotyping. Although slogans may be enlisted to support reasoned ideas, in practice they tend to act only as emotional appeals.
Example: the names of the military campaigns, such as "enduring freedom" or "just cause" can be considered slogans

22. Testimonial

Quotations, in or out of context, especially cited to support or reject a given policy, action, program, or personality.
Example: a person talks about his political agenda, and then uses a study that shows the percentage of satisfaction of his predecessor.

23. Thought-terminating cliché

Thought-terminating clichés, also known as thought-stoppers, are words or phrases that discourage critical thought and meaningful discussion about a given topic. They are typically short, generic sentences that offer seemingly simple answers to complex questions or that distract attention away from other lines of thought. They are often sayings that have been embedded in a culture's folk wisdom
Examples are: "Stop thinking so much", "here we go again"

24. Third party technique

Using a third party as source to provide the information to the audience rather that using someone who’s with a stake in the outcome.
Example: a company with bad reputation tries to publicize its product but uses a journal or news agency to talk about the features of the product on behalf of the company itself

25. Bandwagon

Attempting to persuade the target audience to join in and take the course of action because "everyone else is taking the same action".
Example: All your neighbors are rushing down to Mistri Motors to take advantage of this year-end sale. You come, too

26. Classical conditioning

If A is always present when B is present and B causes a physical reaction (e.g. disgust, pleasure), then when presented with object A in the absence of B, that same reaction will be experienced.
Example (Pavlov's dog): Before learning took place, the dogs would reliably salivate when given meat powder, but they gave no response to the ringing of a bell (neutral). Then Pavlov would always ring a bell just before he would present the dogs with some meat powder. Pretty soon, the dogs began to associate the sound of the bell with the impending presence of meat powder. As a result, they would begin to salivate as soon as they heard the bell, even if it was not immediately followed by the meat powder. In other words, they learned that the bell was a reliable predictor of meat powder.

27. Cognitive Dissonance

Using someones’ else endorsement of a candidate or a product to change people's minds because people cannot tolerate inconsistency.

28. Reductio ad hitlerum

Persuading an audience to disapprove an action or idea by suggesting that the idea is popular with groups hated in contempt by the target audience.
Example: Do you know who else was doing that ? Hitler!
